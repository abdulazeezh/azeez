Research

Sustainable development of the world requires the development of green energy technologies. As the global population is continuously increasing, the need for low-cost and renewable energy solutions are of greater importance. The amount of solar power striking on the earth in an hour is more than annual global power consumption, indicating the potential of Solar cells to meet this demand. Amongst the available solar cell technologies, Organic Solar Cell (OSC) is a promising candidate for green energy demand due to the rousing features such as solution-processability, semitransparency, flexibility and roll to roll printing production. Despite active research, for more than two decades, OSC’s are far from being successfully commercialized as compared to other Photovoltaic (PV) technologies such as Si, because of low efficiency and poor stability. My research broadly focused on understanding the device physics and degradation mechanism of OSC. This understanding is crucial to design strategies, which can simultaneously improve efficiency and stability. Short energy payback time and better indoor performance of OSC’s as compared to the conventional PV technologies demands a parallel establishment of this state-of-the-art technology along with Si photovoltaics.

At present, the most challenging problem is the degradation in OSCs. Consistent performance throughout the lifetime (> 10 years at least) is necessary for this technology to become commercially competitive. Understanding the degradation mechanism will help to identify optimized device architecture, processing conditions and better molecular design strategies. Thus to increase the lifetime of OSCs, a thorough understanding of degradation mechanism in necessary.


Major factors causing degradation in organic solar cells.


Particularly in OSCs the device degradation in the initial period of operation (a few tens to hundred hours) is substantial and is called “burn-in” degradation. After the “burn-in” period (exponential decay in efficiency with time) the performance drops very slowly until the failure of the device. Developing strategies to reduce “burn-in” time and loss is currently of great interest. Apart from this, a well suited flexible and biodegradable plastic substrate for large scale manufacturing of OSCs is needed. Also bridging the gap between performance parameters obtained in the lab and industry is crucial.
